The Titanic: A Legend Born from Tragedy

Do you know the legendary story of the Titanic, the magnificent 'unsinkable' ship that tragically met its fate on its maiden voyage?

  • A Brief Overview

    The Titanic was a British passenger liner, famously known for sinking during its maiden voyage. Built by the White Star Line, it was one of the largest and most luxurious ships ever constructed at the time. The Titanic was advertised as "unsinkable" due to its advanced safety features, including a double hull and 16 watertight compartments. On April 10, 1912, the Titanic set sail from Southampton, England, to New York City with over 2,200 passengers and crew on board. Among the passengers were some of the wealthiest people of the time, as well as immigrants seeking a new life in America.

  • On the night of April 14, 1912, the Titanic struck an iceberg in the North Atlantic Ocean. The iceberg scraped along the ship's starboard side, causing five of its 16 watertight compartments to flood. The ship was not designed to stay afloat with more than four flooded compartments, and as a result, it began to sink. The Titanic had lifeboats for only about half of the people on board, a decision based on outdated maritime regulations. This led to a chaotic evacuation, with many lifeboats launched partially empty. Due to the freezing cold waters and lack of sufficient lifeboats, over 1,500 people died, making it one of the deadliest maritime disasters in history. Most of the deaths were caused by hypothermia in the frigid waters, rather than drowning.

  • The sinking of the Titanic became a symbol of human error, overconfidence in technology, and class disparity. It led to widespread public outrage and resulted in significant changes to maritime safety regulations, including the International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea (SOLAS) in 1914, which mandated sufficient lifeboats and improved safety measures for ships. The wreck of the Titanic was discovered in 1985 on the ocean floor, about 2.5 miles deep, providing further insight into the tragedy and reigniting public fascination with the ship's story.
